An analogy is a similarity of some sort. Similar objects agree with each other in some respect. Analogous objects agree in certain relations of their respective parts.

For example, a parallelogram is analogous to a parallelepiped in two ways: (first we define a "bounding element" of a parallelogram as its edges and the "bounding element" of a parallelepiped as its faces )

  1. Each bounding element is parallel to at most bounding element and adjacent to the rest. Note: the analogy between the two systems consists only in the set of relation(s) defined above.

Simpler analogous problems

Solving a problem by first solving an analogous problem.

Note Two systems can be analogous in some way that is completely irrelevant to the problem at hand. Make sure that analogous relation formed between the two systems work for the problem at hand.

The solution to an analogous problem may not easily extend to the problem at hand. Try to rearrange the solution such that it lends itself better to the problem at hand.

Mathematical Precision

An analogy is a pretty broad idea, but a mathematically precise idea of an analogy can be created with either of the following ideas:

  1. Two systems of mathematical objects, and are so connected that certain relations between objects of are governed by the same laws as those between the objects of . eg: such as in the example of the edges of a triangle to the faces of a tetrahedron.
  2. There is a one-to-one correspondence ([[functions| bijective mapping]]), between two objects of a system preserving certain relations. That is, if such a relation holds between the objects of one system, the same relation holds for the corresponding objects of the other system. This is called an isomorphism.
  3. There is a one-many correspondance between the objects of two systems and preserving certain relations. Such a connection is called a merohedral isomorphism (homomorphism).
